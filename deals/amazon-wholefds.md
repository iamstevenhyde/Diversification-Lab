# Amazon Acquires Whole Foods (2017)

## The Deal
- **Acquirer:** Amazon
- **Target:** Whole Foods Market
- **Price:** $13.7 billion (cash)
- **Premium:** 27% over closing price
- **Date:** June 16, 2017

## Pre-Merger Financials

### Amazon (FY 2016)
- Revenue: $135.99B
- EBITDA: ~$12.5B (9.2% margin)
- AWS Revenue: $12.2B (growing 55%)
- E-commerce grocery share: ~1% of U.S. grocery market
- Prime members: ~65 million

### Whole Foods (FY 2016)
- Revenue: $15.7B
- EBITDA: ~$1.1B (7% margin, declining)
- Stores: 460+ locations
- Comparable store sales: declining for 7 consecutive quarters
- Known as "Whole Paycheck" — premium pricing limiting growth

## Strategic Rationale
- Amazon needed physical distribution for grocery (last mile problem)
- Whole Foods had 460+ premium urban locations
- Grocery is $800B U.S. market — Amazon had barely penetrated it
- Whole Foods was struggling and vulnerable to activist investors

## Integration Type
**Related Diversification** — Amazon's logistics/tech + Whole Foods' physical retail:
- Amazon's supply chain + Whole Foods' store network
- Prime membership + in-store pickup
- Amazon's data analytics + Whole Foods' customer base
- Shared infrastructure: stores became delivery hubs

## Post-Merger Results
- Immediate 30% price cuts on key items at Whole Foods
- Prime member discounts drove foot traffic
- Whole Foods stores became Amazon delivery/pickup hubs
- Amazon Fresh integration with Whole Foods supply chain
- However: grocery market share growth slower than expected
- Store count only grew to ~500 by 2023 (modest expansion)

## Discussion Questions
1. Was this related diversification or vertical integration? (Could argue both)
2. What shared capabilities justify owning vs. just partnering?
3. At $13.7B, does the cost-of-entry test pass? Calculate the NPV of synergies needed.
4. Why has Amazon's grocery market share grown slower than expected?
