# Disney Acquires Pixar (2006)

## The Deal
- **Acquirer:** The Walt Disney Company
- **Target:** Pixar Animation Studios
- **Price:** $7.4 billion (all stock)
- **Premium:** ~4% over market price
- **Date:** January 24, 2006

## Pre-Merger Financials

### Disney (FY 2005)
- Revenue: $31.9B
- EBITDA: ~$5.7B (17.9% margin)
- Studio Entertainment Revenue: $7.6B
- Animation track record: Chicken Little ($314M global), Home on the Range ($104M — flop)

### Pixar (FY 2005)
- Revenue: $273M
- EBITDA: ~$137M (50% margin)
- Films: 6 for 6 hits (Toy Story through The Incredibles)
- Average global box office per film: ~$550M

## Strategic Rationale
- Disney's internal animation had declined (post-Lion King era)
- Pixar had the creative talent + technology (RenderMan)
- Existing distribution deal was expiring — Pixar could walk
- Steve Jobs became Disney's largest individual shareholder

## Integration Type
**Related Diversification** — Both in entertainment/animation, but Pixar brought:
- Superior CGI technology (shared across Disney parks, products)
- Creative culture that Disney had lost
- Talent pipeline (John Lasseter became Chief Creative Officer)

## Post-Merger Results
- Pixar films post-acquisition: WALL-E, Up, Toy Story 3, Inside Out, Coco, Soul
- Combined animation revenue grew from $7.6B to $11.1B by 2010
- Pixar technology deployed across Disney parks and Disney+ content pipeline
- Estimated synergy value: $1-2B annually in cross-platform revenue

## Discussion Questions
1. Does this pass the better-off test? Could Disney have just renewed the distribution deal?
2. What capabilities transferred between the two companies?
3. Was $7.4B justified given Pixar's $273M revenue?
4. What integration risks did Disney face? How did they manage creative culture?
